# Write a PRD That Travels

> Use the One-Breath Rule to simplify your problem statement, solution, and success metric into something clear enough to lead with in every meeting.

## The Gap

PMs spend 4 weeks writing PRDs when they could spend 48 hours, because they write chronologically instead of backwards from the decision. All PMs document what they're building. Great PMs distill the problem, solution, and impact into one sentence—then build everything else around that clarity.

The One-Breath Rule works like this: explain the problem, the solution, and the impact in one breath. Out loud. If you can't, keep iterating. What sounds like one problem is usually three. The "solution" is usually a feature list, not a path. The impact is vague—a metric without a timeframe, a benefit without a measure.

## Why It Matters

Simplicity signals seniority. When you confuse your team about what you're building and why, decisions slow down. Cross-functional partners ask clarifying questions instead of shipping. Your leadership spends meetings on interpretation instead of judgment. The PRD becomes a document people reference, not a north star that organizes decisions.

PMs often equate rigor with volume—bringing multiple problems into the room, describing solutions as feature lists, framing impact in ways that sound good but resist measurement. They add more when the real work is distilling. A PRD with three clear problems beats a PRD with ten vague ones. A one-sentence solution beats five features. Once you can say it in one breath, that becomes your headline. Lead with it in every meeting, every doc, every email.

## The Framework

1. **The problem** (1 sentence: whose problem, what friction, what it costs)
2. **The solution** (1 sentence: what you're building and for whom)
3. **The success metric** (1 measurable outcome with timeframe)
4. **Why now** (what's changed that makes this the right moment)
5. **What you're NOT doing** (explicit scope limits—just as important as what you are)
6. **Key open questions** (what still needs resolving before you commit)

Write the approval you need first. Then work backwards to the evidence that supports it. One problem. One solution. One measurable impact. Everything else is support.

## Common Mistakes

- **Conflating multiple problems.** "Users struggle with onboarding and retention" is two problems wearing a trench coat. Separate them.
- **Writing solutions as feature lists.** "Add notification settings, improve help text, and redesign the flow" is not a solution. It's a list. A solution is the integrated path that solves the problem.
- **Vague metrics.** "Improve retention" doesn't measure. "Increase 30-day retention by 8% for new users in the US" does.
- **Leading with why you want to build it, not why users need it.** Your motivation isn't the problem.
- **Leaving scope fuzzy.** "Not doing" is your friend. It clarifies what others shouldn't expect.

## Try This Week

Take a PRD or initiative you're working on. Set a timer for 2 minutes. Say the problem, solution, and impact out loud—one breath. Where did you stumble? That's where clarity is missing. Don't move forward until you can say it smoothly. Then use that one sentence as the opening of every sync and async discussion about this work.
