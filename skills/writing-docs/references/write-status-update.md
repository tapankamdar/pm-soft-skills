# Write Status Updates That Are Read

> Use the 3-2-1 Framework to write transparent, outcome-focused updates that leaders actually read.

## The Gap

Most status reports get ignored because they're written for yourself, not for your reader. You include everything—tasks, blockers, minutiae—when your leader needs to know what progressed and whether you need help. All PMs report progress. Great PMs communicate outcomes that matter to the business and the user.

At Meta, teams spent significant effort on their weekly Highlights/Progress/Me reports, but leaders barely read them. The problem wasn't effort—it was that the updates answered the PM's questions, not the leader's. As one GoDaddy CFO said to a PM: "I have not read a status update you sent in the past 3 months. Until today."

## Why It Matters

A status update that isn't read doesn't provide transparency. It doesn't demonstrate accountability. And it definitely doesn't align teams. When leaders can't trust that your update tells them what they need to know, they build their own information systems—asking your team directly, requesting additional meetings, sending you follow-up questions. You create noise instead of clarity.

The real cost is credibility. Leaders notice which PMs they have to chase for context and which ones proactively frame the situation. Over time, PMs who communicate clearly get more autonomy. Those who don't get more oversight.

## The Framework

**3 Updates and Outcomes** (progress with metrics—absolute and %)
- Each bullet focuses on value delivered, not tasks completed.
- Every bullet is under 100 words.
- Every bullet includes metrics that measure impact.

**2 Upcoming Milestones** (with green/yellow/red status)
- These show momentum ahead.
- Status signals are explicit—no ambiguity.

**1 Help Needed** (with a specific person's name + context, not a team name)
- This is how you unblock. Be specific about what you need and from whom.
- You can add a second bullet by exception only.

**The Summary** (max 200 words)
- This is your opinionated POV as a leader, separate from the raw updates.
- Answer: "After reading this, does my reader know what to do or think differently?"

## Common Mistakes

- **Task-focused bullets.** "Conducted user research" is not an outcome. "User research revealed 40% of users abandon checkout at payment—pursuing simplified flow" is.
- **Vague help requests.** "Need design input from Design" is invisible. "Need Jane's input on whether we should simplify the checkout flow now or after Q2 launch" is specific.
- **Missing metrics.** "Increased engagement" doesn't travel. "Increased engagement by 12% (from 34% to 46%) week-over-week" does.
- **Burying the lede.** Your summary should appear first, then the framework. Don't make leaders hunt for what matters.
- **No status on milestones.** Yellow and red statuses matter. They trigger help before crisis. Don't hide them.

## Try This Week

Pick one weekly update you're about to send. Before hitting send, rewrite it using the 3-2-1 framework. Include one real metric per outcome. Name the specific person you need help from. Read the summary out loud—does it make sense to someone who hasn't been in every meeting? If not, edit until it does.
