# Manage Up: The Playbook for Leaders

> Building influence with your manager isn't about sucking up. It's about making their job easier so you can amplify your impact.

## The Gap

If you think your boss is the problem, it's probably you. All PMs report to someone. Great PMs shift their mindset: your manager's success is your success. Their goals directly influence your team's direction and your own autonomy.

Early in your career, you might believe managing up is unnecessary—that you can keep your head down and let your work speak for itself. It doesn't work that way. Without influence with your manager, you're always reacting to direction instead of shaping it.

## Why It Matters

Your manager is the gateway to your impact. They allocate resources, set priorities, shape how executives see your work, and decide how much autonomy you get. When you build trust with them, you move faster. When you don't, you hit friction on every major decision.

The PM who waits for instructions creates dependency. The PM who proactively aligns with their manager's goals becomes a thought partner. Over time, this person gets more latitude, better projects, and higher trust. The pattern compounds.

## The Framework

**10 Principles for Managing Up:**

**1. Shift your mindset.** Your manager's success is your success. Stop waiting for instructions. Align with their goals and bring actionable solutions.

**2. Communicate proactively.** Don't make your manager chase you for updates. At Mozilla, a weekly "progress-outcomes-need help" newsletter built trust and independence. Say: "Here's a quick update: [key project] is on track, and I've flagged [specific risk] with a proposed solution below."

**3. Learn their style.** Every leader has unique preferences. One manager thrives on data but hates long decks. Present key insights in bullet points upfront, with deep-dive slides behind for those who want detail.

**4. Bring solutions, not just problems.** When an experiment faced delays, outline three potential pivots with pros, cons, and your recommendation. The manager chose one—but what mattered was showing initiative to drive progress. Say: "This process is causing delays because of [specific issue]. Let's try [proposed solution]."

**5. Clarify expectations upfront.** Ask: "Here's my understanding of what success looks like: [specific details]. Does this align with your vision?"

**6. Master framing.** Same message, different delivery, very different result. At Mozilla, roadmap changes got buy-in when framed around growth strategy, not just internal preference.

**7. Adapt to their weak spots.** If your manager struggles with prioritization, create a decision matrix. You become the force multiplier by addressing blind spots proactively.

**8. Be a force multiplier.** Take ownership of key initiatives so your manager can focus on executive-level priorities. Say: "I noticed this initiative could use additional support. I'd happily take ownership to free up your bandwidth."

**9. Build trust through follow-through.** At an early-stage startup, committed to a launch in six weeks. Hit unexpected hurdles, flagged them immediately, proposed a revised timeline. That consistency builds credibility.

**10. Know when to push back.** At Meta, a leader proposed integrating a feature across all platforms simultaneously. Pushed back with data showing a phased approach would be smoother. The leader agreed. Say: "I see the value in exploring this idea, but I'm concerned it might take focus away from higher-priority initiatives. Can we discuss how it fits into the bigger picture?"

## Common Mistakes

- **Hiding bad news.** Tell your manager early, with proposed solutions.
- **Asking for permission on everything.** Managers want you to solve problems, not escalate them all.
- **Assuming they know what you're doing.** Proactive communication saves them time and builds their confidence in you.
- **Treating your manager like the problem.** If you're stuck, ask yourself: What does my manager need from me? How can I help them succeed?
- **Not adapting to how they work.** Some managers want frequent syncs. Others want fewer, longer conversations. Match their style.

## Try This Week

Schedule a 1:1 with your manager. Ask: "What are your biggest priorities right now? What's keeping you up at night? Where can I help?" Listen without solving. Then come back with one specific way you can lighten their load this quarter. Watch how the relationship shifts.
