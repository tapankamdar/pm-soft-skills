# Earn Trust When Things Go Wrong

> Trust is earned in the gaps: when you're behind, when things go wrong, when updates are hard. Don't hide. Communicate.

## The Gap

Most PMs communicate well when things are going well. The real test of trust is what happens when a milestone slips, a launch gets delayed, or a decision was wrong. In those moments, the instinct is to minimize, delay, or over-qualify. That instinct is wrong.

> "Every roadmap decision builds trust or breaks it. Say yes too fast, you disappoint later. Say nothing when it slips, you lose credibility. Trust is earned in the gaps." — Tapan Kamdar

## Why It Matters

People don't need perfection from a PM. They need honesty. When stakeholders feel that you'll tell them the truth even when it's uncomfortable, they extend you latitude — more time, more budget, more benefit of the doubt. When they suspect you're hiding or spinning, they start building parallel information systems. They go around you. They stop trusting your recommendations.

Trust compounds. Built incrementally over many small moments, it becomes the resource that allows you to take bigger bets, ship faster, and lead through ambiguity. Damaged in one public moment, it takes months to rebuild.

## The Framework: Communicate Through Hard Moments

When things go wrong, there are four things your stakeholders need from you — in this order:

**1. Acknowledge early, not late.**
The instinct is to wait until you have a solution before sharing a problem. Reverse this. Surface the risk as soon as you see it, not after it becomes a crisis. Being first with bad news is far better than being the last.

**2. Be specific about the gap.**
"We're behind" is not useful. "The feature ships in two weeks instead of one, because X took longer than planned" is actionable. Name the root cause. Avoid euphemisms.

**3. Own the decision or the miss.**
Don't distribute blame. Even when a delay was caused by a dependency outside your control, your job is to have flagged it earlier. Take accountability for the state of things, not just your own actions.

**4. Reset expectations with a new plan.**
Don't just report the problem — give a revised picture. "Here's where we are, here's what we're doing, here's the new milestone." Give your stakeholder something to hold.

## The Trust Equation

Trust = Competence × Consistency × Candor

You can be highly competent and still lose trust if you're inconsistent (sometimes forthcoming, sometimes evasive) or low on candor (you tell people what they want to hear). All three factors matter.

**Candor** means saying the hard thing, not the comfortable thing. It means telling leadership there's a risk before they ask. It means saying "I got this wrong" rather than "things didn't go as planned."

## Resetting Expectations Without Losing Credibility

When you have to reset a commitment:
1. Lead with the change: "I need to update you on the timeline for X."
2. Explain the root cause briefly and honestly.
3. State the new plan and timeline.
4. If appropriate, describe what you're doing to prevent the same miss.
5. Ask: "Do you have concerns about this change, or is there anything you need from me?"

Do this before they ask. Do it in writing so there's a clear record. Don't bury it in a meeting agenda.

## Common Mistakes

- **Waiting for certainty before communicating.** A risk surfaced early is far less damaging than a surprise.
- **Hiding behind passive language.** "The timeline shifted" is not the same as "we need more time."
- **Communicating only to your manager.** Make sure the right stakeholders — not just your direct chain — know what's happening.
- **Focusing on what you did instead of what you need.** Your audience wants a plan, not a defense.

## Try This Week

Identify one risk or slip on your current work that you haven't communicated yet. Write a two-paragraph update using the framework above: what's the gap, what's the cause, what's the new plan. Send it before it becomes a crisis.
