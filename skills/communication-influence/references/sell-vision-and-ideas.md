# Sell Your Vision and Ideas

> All PMs communicate. Great PMs sell belief. The job isn't to inform — it's to move people.

## The Gap

Most PMs think of their job as building and shipping. But the real job starts before a single line of code is written: you have to get people to believe in the problem, trust your approach, and want to help. You are constantly selling.

> "PMs have two jobs: Communicate and Sell. Sell the vision. Sell the ideas that make the vision real. Sell the impact to every XFN and leader. Then communicate the vision, the roadmap, the progress, the launch, the feedback, the outcome. Then sell the vision again — now with results." — Tapan Kamdar

## Why It Matters

Ideas die quietly when they are communicated, not sold. When you present a roadmap slide, you're informing. When you make someone feel the problem your users have — the frustration, the workaround, the wasted time — you are selling. One creates awareness. The other creates energy.

The best PM ideas win not because they were technically superior but because someone made them feel necessary. Engineering teams work harder for problems they believe in. Executives approve budgets for visions they can see. Cross-functional partners go the extra mile when they understand the stakes.

Selling is not manipulation. It is clarity of conviction — and the skill to help others share it.

## What You Are Selling

**The vision** — The world you are trying to create for your users. Make it specific enough to be believable, inspiring enough to matter.

**The problem** — The pain that makes the vision urgent. Not "users are unhappy" — paint the specific moment of frustration. Give it a face. Give it a name.

**The idea** — Not a feature list. A thesis: if we do X, Y will change for Z type of user. One clear causal chain.

**The impact** — The before and after. What does a user experience today that they won't after your work? What does the business gain?

**Your results** — Once you ship, you sell again. "We did X and here is what changed." This is how credibility compounds.

## The Sell Tour

Before any major roadmap decision, new initiative, or significant feature, run a Sell Tour:

1. **Identify every stakeholder** whose belief you need — not just their approval.
2. **Meet them individually first.** Understand their framing. Speak their language.
3. **Lead with the problem**, not the solution. Ask if they feel it too.
4. **Show the impact** in terms that matter to them — user impact, business impact, team velocity.
5. **Handle objections before the room.** Know the "yes, but..." before you're in public.

By the time you're in the formal meeting, it's not a debate. It's a confirmation.

## Common Mistakes

- **Presenting, not selling.** A deck full of data is not a pitch. People don't rally around requirements; they rally around problems.
- **Waiting for the meeting.** If your first real conversation about a big idea is in the review meeting, you are already behind.
- **Selling to the wrong room.** Know which person's "yes" unlocks everyone else's. Go there first.
- **Stopping after launch.** The "communicate the outcome" cycle is how you earn trust for the next vision. Close the loop with data.

## Try This Week

Take your current top initiative. Write the one-sentence problem statement (not the solution). Then write the one-sentence impact statement (what changes for the user if this works). Share those two sentences with someone outside your team and ask: "Does this feel important?" Their reaction will tell you whether you're selling or reporting.
