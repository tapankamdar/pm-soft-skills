# Simplify for Clarity

> If you can't say it simply, you haven't thought it through. Clarity is hard. It is also your job.

## The Gap

PMs often equate complexity with rigor. Long documents, jargon-heavy language, multi-layered frameworks — they feel like evidence of thorough thinking. But in practice, complexity creates friction. When people can't follow you, they disengage. When they disengage, they don't buy in.

> "Complexity is easy. Clarity is hard. Great PMs make hard things legible: Problem. Path. Payoff." — Tapan Kamdar

## Why It Matters

In any cross-functional environment, your ideas will be evaluated at the speed of understanding. If a design partner, an engineering lead, or a senior executive can't quickly grasp what you're proposing — the problem, the approach, the expected outcome — they will default to doubt.

Clarity is not dumbing things down. It is doing the intellectual work so others don't have to. It is a sign of respect for the reader's time and a demonstration of how deeply you understand the problem.

When you are the clearest voice in the room, people follow you. When you are the most complex, they ask for clarification — and then move on.

## The Problem-Path-Payoff Framework

Every PM communication — email, meeting, document, Slack message — should be answerable in three beats:

**Problem** — What is the actual problem? Not the solution, not the feature. The friction a user feels or the gap in the business. State it in one sentence.

**Path** — What is the approach? One clear path, not six options. Show your work, but lead with the direction. If there are tradeoffs, name them — but don't hide behind them.

**Payoff** — What changes if we do this? What does success look like, in concrete terms? What does the user experience that they don't today?

If you can answer all three in under 90 seconds — spoken or written — you have clarity. If you can't, keep thinking.

## The Jargon Test

Read your last document. Circle every word or phrase that requires context only someone on your team would have. Rewrite those sentences as if you were explaining the idea to a smart friend outside the company.

If the meaning survives the rewrite, the jargon was unnecessary. If the meaning changes, you may have been hiding behind the language.

## Common Mistakes

- **Leading with solutions.** Most people lead with what they want to build. Lead with the problem instead. It changes the conversation from "should we build this?" to "yes, how do we solve this?"
- **Using hedge language.** "There may be potential opportunity to..." is not a point of view. "We should..." is. Be direct.
- **Equating length with quality.** A 2-page clear document is more valuable than a 20-page dense one. Cut until it hurts, then cut once more.
- **Making the audience do the work.** Don't give people data and ask them to draw their own conclusions. Give the conclusion, then show the evidence.

## Try This Week

Take something you're about to send — a spec, an email, a meeting agenda. Apply the Problem-Path-Payoff filter. Cut any sentence that doesn't serve one of the three. Read it out loud. If a sentence takes more than one breath, rewrite it shorter.
