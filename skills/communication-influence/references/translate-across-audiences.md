# Translate Across Audiences

> Great PMs don't broadcast. They bridge. They speak the language of each listener.

## The Gap

Most PMs say the same thing the same way to every room. They send the same roadmap slide to engineering and leadership. They write the same summary for their manager and for the design team. Then they wonder why people aren't engaged, aren't aligned, or keep asking the same questions.

The problem is not the message. It's that every audience speaks a different language — and cares about different things.

> "PMs are like C-3PO. They speak many languages. Engineering, design, data, marketing, executives. Each group hears differently. Each group cares about different things. Great PMs translate." — Tapan Kamdar

## Why It Matters

Alignment breaks down at the translation layer, not the information layer. You can have perfect data and still lose the room if you present it in engineering terms to a marketing leader, or business-case language to a design team that cares about the user.

When you speak your listener's language, they stop defending and start contributing. They see themselves in the vision. They understand what you need from them — and why it matters to them personally.

## The Audiences and What They Care About

**Engineering** — They want clarity, constraints, and confidence that requirements are real. Speak in specifics: edge cases, technical tradeoffs, explicit success criteria. Avoid vague business-speak. Honor their skepticism.

**Design** — They want the user's problem, not the solution. Lead with empathy: who is struggling, what does the current experience feel like, what does "better" actually mean. Don't prescribe the pixels.

**Data and Analytics** — They want a clear question, not a request to "look into it." Give them a hypothesis. Tell them what decision hinges on the answer. Be explicit about what you need and by when.

**Marketing** — They want the story: the before, the transformation, the proof point. Lead with the user's world. Help them see the hook before you talk about the feature.

**Executives** — They want the business case, the risk, and the ask. Lead with the outcome, then the path. Put the recommendation first. Anticipate objections before they ask.

## The Translation Test

Before any communication — a Slack message, a doc, a meeting agenda, a presentation — ask: "Does this speak the language of the person reading it?"

- What do they care about most right now?
- What question are they trying to answer?
- What would make them feel confident, not just informed?

Rewrite with that lens. You will say less. It will land more.

## Common Mistakes

- **Sending one doc to everyone.** The same PRD should not go to engineering, design, and leadership unchanged. Tailor the intro and framing for each audience.
- **Assuming shared context.** Every audience has a different mental model of your product. Don't assume they know why the problem matters.
- **Over-explaining to executives.** They don't need the backstory. They need the recommendation and the tradeoff.
- **Under-explaining to engineers.** They need the "why" so they can make good judgment calls during implementation.

## Try This Week

Take your next major update or doc. Before you send it, identify the primary audience. Ask: what question are they trying to answer? Rewrite the opening paragraph for that question. Test whether the framing shifts — it almost always will.
