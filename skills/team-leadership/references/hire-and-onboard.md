# Hire Great PMs and Onboard Them Well

> The highest-leverage thing you can do for your product is hire the right person. The second highest is making sure they succeed.

## The Gap

Most PM hiring processes are reactive: a headcount opens, a job description is copied from the last time, interviews are scheduled, and someone is selected based on who felt best in the room. This approach systematically underperforms because it optimizes for chemistry over fit, and activity over outcomes.

Great hiring is a product problem. You have a user (the candidate), a market (the talent pool), and a goal (a PM who can solve your specific problem). Treat it with the same rigor you bring to your product.

## Why It Matters

A bad hire does not just fail to contribute. They create drag: misaligned decisions, diminished team energy, the management overhead of remediation. A great hire multiplies everyone around them. The difference in outcome between an average and exceptional PM is not 20% — it is 10x over a two-year horizon.

Onboarding is often where the investment is lost. Hiring the right person and then leaving them to figure out the context, the culture, and the expectations by themselves is one of the most common and costly PM management mistakes.

## What You Are Hiring For

Before you post a role, define the job in terms of outcomes, not activities.

Ask: "What does success look like for this person in 90 days, 6 months, and 12 months?" Write those down. They become your interview criteria, your onboarding plan, and your first performance conversation framework.

The three dimensions that matter most for PM hires:

**Judgment** — Can they figure out the right problem to solve? Can they navigate ambiguity without needing constant direction? Can they make the call when the data is incomplete?

**Communication** — Can they earn alignment across engineering, design, leadership, and cross-functional partners? Do they write clearly, present with conviction, and listen before they speak?

**Ownership** — Will they take the product personally? Do they have a track record of creating outcomes, not just shipping features?

## The Hiring Process That Works

**1. Write a specific job description.**
Most job descriptions are a list of generic requirements. Instead, write the job as a problem statement: "We are building X, we have Y challenge, and we need someone who can do Z." Specific descriptions attract better candidates.

**2. Design structured interviews.**
Every interviewer should know their question domain and have a rubric. Cover: product sense, execution, communication, leadership (scaled to level). Avoid "tell me about a time you built something." Ask for the decision, the tradeoff, the learning.

**3. Use a work sample or case.**
Ask candidates to do a version of the actual work: a brief product analysis, a problem statement, or a strategy question relevant to your space. This predicts performance better than most interview questions.

**4. Check references purposefully.**
Don't treat references as a formality. Ask: "What environment does this person thrive in? When have they struggled? What would make this role a great fit — or a hard one?" You'll learn more in 15 minutes of a real reference conversation than in most interviews.

## The 30-60-90 Onboarding Plan

A new PM needs three things in their first 90 days: context, relationships, and early wins.

**First 30 days — Learn:**
- Deep dive into the product, users, data, and team.
- 1:1s with all key stakeholders (engineering, design, data, marketing, leadership).
- No decisions yet. Observe, ask questions, listen.

**Days 31-60 — Contribute:**
- Take ownership of one defined problem or initiative.
- Begin leading ceremonies (standups, reviews) in your area.
- Share a written "what I've learned and what I think" document with your manager. This forces synthesis and surfaces misalignments early.

**Days 61-90 — Lead:**
- Drive a decision — a prioritization call, a scope tradeoff, a go/no-go.
- Establish your communication rhythm (status updates, roadmap reviews).
- Set goals for the next quarter aligned to the team's objectives.

## Common Mistakes

- **Hiring for pedigree over craft.** A PM from a famous company who can't articulate a user problem or make a hard tradeoff will not perform. Evaluate for the work itself.
- **Skipping the onboarding plan.** A new PM without a clear first-90-days structure defaults to what they knew at their last job — which may not fit your context.
- **Waiting too long to give feedback.** If a new PM is heading in the wrong direction, tell them in week 3, not month 4. Early redirection is kind; late redirection is a crisis.

## Try This Week

If you're preparing to hire: write the success criteria for the role before writing the job description. If you recently hired: review your new PM's first 30 days against the learn-contribute-lead framework. What context do they still need?
