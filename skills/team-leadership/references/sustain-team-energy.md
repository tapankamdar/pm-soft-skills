# Sustain Team Energy

> Deadlines matter. But energy matters more. Features are outputs. Team energy is fuel.

## The Gap

PMs track Jira boards, burn charts, and velocity. These tell you what shipped — but not whether your team has the will to keep going. The most dangerous state for a product team is not being behind on a deadline. It is being burned out, going through the motions, or losing the spark that made the work meaningful.

> "PMs track momentum. They feel the vibe. They read the room. Are we excited or drained? Are we stuck or flowing? Jira doesn't tell you that. Burn charts don't show that. A great PM knows when to push and when to pause." — Tapan Kamdar

## Why It Matters

High-performing teams are not high-performing all the time. They are high-performing at the right times — because someone protected their energy when it mattered. The PM's job is not just to plan the work. It is to manage the conditions under which the work gets done.

A team with great energy ships better work faster. A team in depletion ships reluctant features with subtle bugs and low morale. The difference compounds over months.

## Reading Team Energy

Energy is harder to measure than velocity, but it is observable. Signs that energy is high:
- People are proactively raising issues before they become blockers.
- Conversations are generative — adding ideas, not just defending positions.
- People ask questions that show curiosity, not just compliance.

Signs that energy is dropping:
- Standup updates are minimal and defensive.
- Blockers stay in place longer than usual.
- People stop volunteering for stretch work.
- Side conversations shift from ideas to complaints.

When you notice the signals, act on them. Don't wait for a crisis.

## The Energy Equation

> Energy = Clarity × Autonomy × Progress × Recognition

**Clarity** — Does the team know why the work matters? Do they understand the user and the outcome? Confusion is energetically expensive. Clarity is free.

**Autonomy** — Are people making meaningful decisions in their area, or are they executing a prescription? Ownership energizes. Being micromanaged depletes.

**Progress** — Can people see that their work is moving something? Invisible progress kills motivation. Make wins visible: celebrate shipped features, share user feedback, highlight metric movement.

**Recognition** — Do people feel seen? Not just their output — their craft, their judgment, their extra effort? Recognition does not require a ceremony. A specific, sincere acknowledgment lands more than a generic "great job."

If any one of these drops, momentum stalls. Identify which one is low before assuming you need a team offsite.

## Knowing When to Push and When to Pause

**Push when:**
- The deadline matters and the team has the energy for it.
- There is a clear goal that creates its own urgency.
- The team is in flow — don't interrupt it.

**Pause when:**
- The same people have been sprinting for more than 6 weeks without a break.
- Quality is declining (bugs, shortcuts, arguments about edge cases that would normally be obvious).
- You sense that people are losing confidence in the direction.

A well-timed pause — a one-day team offsite, a "no meetings Friday," a retrospective that actually creates change — restores more energy than it costs.

## Common Mistakes

- **Treating morale as a soft concern.** Team energy directly affects output. It is a product risk, not an HR issue.
- **Assuming people will tell you when they're burned out.** They often won't until it's too late. Read the signals.
- **Using social events as a substitute for structural fixes.** A team dinner does not fix unclear goals or misaligned priorities. Diagnose first.
- **Celebrating only launches.** Recognize the work that made the launch possible — the hard week of debugging, the design iteration, the research that shifted the direction.

## Try This Week

In your next team interaction — a standup, a 1:1, a sync — stop and actually read the room. Ask yourself: is this team energized or depleted right now? If depleted, what's one thing you could do this week — a win to celebrate, a blocker to remove, a piece of clarity to provide — that would restore a bit of that energy?
