# Run Productive Meetings

> Win before you enter the room. The best influence happens in the preparation, not the presentation.

## The Gap

The cost of a bad meeting isn't just the time in the room. It's the momentum killed, the trust eroded, and the decisions that don't get made. All PMs schedule decision meetings. Great PMs get decisions made before the meeting starts.

There are three types of people in a meeting: those who need to decide, those who need to inform, those who just want to be in the room. Only invite the first two. At GoDaddy, a PM walked into a meeting with five executive leaders to make a big decision. Five minutes in, the decision was made. One leader said: "That was the easiest decision I've made all week." What most people didn't see was the hours spent behind the scenes.

## Why It Matters

Meetings that feel like debates are expensive. They kill momentum. They make people defensive. They produce decisions that feel imposed, not owned. The alternative is deciding before the meeting—quietly, in 1:1s where objections surface without judgment, in pre-reads that frame the narrative, in follow-ups that tie alignment together.

Surprise kills trust. Silence kills support. When people walk into a meeting and hear something unexpected, they close ranks and debate. When they've already been heard, they confirm.

## The Framework

**Rules for productive large meetings:**

1. **One owner.** The person accountable for the outcome. That's it.

2. **One decision or output.** If there's no decision to be made or deliverable to produce, don't meet. Send it async. The meeting must have a point.

3. **Agenda 24 hours in advance.** If it doesn't go out, cancel and reschedule. People need time to think.

4. **First 5 minutes are context. Next 50 are work.** Get aligned on what you're solving, then solve it.

5. **Disagreement in the room is fine. Unresolved disagreement leaving the room is not.** Before people leave, you have alignment or a clear escalation path.

6. **End with clarity.** What was decided? Who owns what? By when? Write it. Confirm it. Move on.

**Before the meeting even starts:**

- Identify all decision-makers and key influencers.
- Book 1:1s with each. Listen. What are they trying to unlock? What are their concerns?
- Write a pre-read based on what you heard—address the real objections, not the ones you assumed.
- Share the pre-read 24-48 hours before. The goal: they arrive informed, not surprised.
- In the meeting, confirm what's already aligned. Don't re-debate what's decided.

## Common Mistakes

- **Inviting everyone.** You dilute the decision and extend the debate.
- **No clear owner.** Meetings with no owner finish with "let's keep talking about this."
- **Surprising the room.** You bring new information in the meeting instead of the pre-read. Now you're starting over.
- **Leaving without resolution.** "We'll circle back" kills momentum and trust.
- **No pre-read or unclear agenda.** People show up unprepared, which turns a decision into a discussion.

## Try This Week

Before your next large meeting, book one 1:1 with the decision-maker. Listen. Ask what they're concerned about. Then write a two-page pre-read that addresses those concerns directly. Send it 24 hours before the meeting. When you walk in, you'll notice the difference. The room will be ready to confirm, not debate.
