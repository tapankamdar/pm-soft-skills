# Make a Product Decision in 30 Minutes (Not 30 Hours)

> Use when you're facing a critical decision and need to cut through endless debate and get to a call.

## The Gap

All PMs make product decisions. Great PMs make them fast without sacrificing quality.

Most product decisions take too long because the decision criteria aren't defined upfront. Without criteria, you'll debate forever. Someone will argue for speed, someone else for quality, someone else for market reach. You're all right. You just haven't agreed on what matters most.

## Why It Matters

Decision paralysis is real. Every week you don't decide is a week your team spends in planning mode instead of building mode. Momentum suffers. Uncertainty spreads. People start making micro-decisions to fill the void — and those scattered decisions lead to incoherent products.

But rushing is also real. A bad decision made fast is worse than a good decision made thoughtfully.

The fix is neither speed nor thoughtfulness. It's clarity. When you define the decision criteria upfront, speed and quality align. You spend your time evaluating options against what matters, not debating what should matter.

## The Framework

Use this 30-minute structure:

**Minute 1-5: State the Decision Clearly**

Write it down in one sentence. "Do we build, buy, or partner for our data pipeline capability?" or "Do we launch with these three features or add two more?" Be specific. If you can't state it in a sentence, you're trying to make two decisions.

**Minute 6-10: List Your Options**

What are the 2-3 realistic paths? For build vs. buy vs. partner, the options are those three. For scope, it might be "launch now with features A-B-C" or "slip 6 weeks and add D and E." Be realistic. Don't list 10 options.

**Minute 11-20: Define Your Criteria**

What would make this a good decision? Write 2-3 criteria that matter for *this* decision:
- Speed to market?
- Quality/reliability?
- Cost?
- Team capability and growth?
- Differentiation vs. commodity?

Not all criteria matter equally. Rank them. "Speed is 1x, quality is 2x, cost is 0.5x" — use weights if it helps.

If your team disagrees on criteria, you haven't aligned yet. Stop. Align on criteria first. Once you agree on what matters, the decision is often obvious.

**Minute 21-29: Score Options Against Criteria**

For each option, ask: How does it stack against each criterion? Build scores: low/medium/high or 1-5. Use AI to help you scenario-plan tradeoffs fast. "Given these options and these criteria, what are the tradeoffs?" AI will surface the nuances quickly.

**Minute 30: Make the Call**

Pick the option that scores highest against your weighted criteria. If it's close, your criteria might be wrong. But don't debate forever. Close it.

## Special Case: Build vs. Buy vs. Partner

- **Build:** when the capability is core differentiation and you have the skills.
- **Buy:** when you need speed and the capability is non-core. You're not trying to be the best in the world at data pipelines; you're trying to use a good one to build faster.
- **Partner:** when you need reach or expertise you can't build quickly. You don't have the skill in-house and building it will take 6 months.

The mistake: defaulting to "build" because it feels more product-y. Build is the right choice only when it meets your criteria, not when it feels good.

## Common Mistakes

- **Debating criteria instead of defining them upfront.** This is where time evaporates. Define criteria before you evaluate options.
- **Changing criteria mid-stream to favor your preferred option.** If you catch yourself doing this, it's a sign you don't actually believe in your preferred option.
- **Treating all criteria equally.** Speed and quality don't matter the same. Rank them. "We'd rather ship slow and right than fast and broken."
- **Making decisions without documenting the criteria.** Three months later, someone will ask "why didn't we buy?" Write it down: "We chose to build because we need differentiation, we have the team, and we value learning over speed for this capability."
- **Assuming the team understands the decision after you make it.** Communicate the decision, the criteria, and the tradeoffs. Sell it. That's what decision-making is.

## Try This Week

Take one decision you've been sitting with. Write it down in one sentence. List your options. Define your 2-3 criteria and rank them. Use that framework to evaluate your options.

You'll either reach a clear call in 30 minutes or you'll realize you need more information on a specific criterion. Either way, you've cut through the fog.
