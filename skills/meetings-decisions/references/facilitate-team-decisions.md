# Facilitate Team Decisions With Better Questions

> Stop answering. Start leading. The best PMs are learning engines, not answer machines.

## The Gap

All PMs have answers. Great PMs ask questions. You're not expected to have all the answers. You're expected to ask better questions that surface what your team actually knows, unlock thinking, and move the room toward clarity.

At Meta, during a tense product review where a dozen leaders debated features, timelines, and tradeoffs, the conversation was spiraling. Then one PM quietly asked: "What problem are we actually solving?" Silence. Then a reset. The room shifted from debating solutions to aligning on purpose. One question cut through noise, ego, and urgency, bringing everyone back to first principles.

## Why It Matters

When you give the answer, you make the decision. The team executes it, but they don't own it. When you ask the question, the team makes the decision—and owns the outcome.

The PM who always answers creates dependency. The team comes to you for every decision. You become the bottleneck. The PM who asks questions builds capability. The team learns to think. They own the work. They move faster without you.

PMs often equate being smart with having answers. But the smartest move is being the editor, not the expert. Not the loudest voice. Not the fastest answer. But the clearest thought. The sharpest question.

## The Framework

**Better questions that unlock a room:**

**"What problem are we actually solving?"**
This cuts through feature-focused debates and resets on purpose. People stop arguing about implementation and start aligning on the real issue.

**"What's the user trying to do?"**
Brings the person back into focus when the room drifts into process or politics.

**"What would change our minds about this?"**
Surface the criteria for a good decision. Once people articulate what would change their mind, you know what to test, research, or learn.

**"What's the one thing that has to be true for this to work?"**
This reveals the hidden dependencies. The assumption everyone's making but not saying. Address that one thing and half your obstacles disappear.

**"What information would make this decision obvious?"**
Ends circular debates. Stops the "let me give my opinion" pattern. Shifts to "here's what we need to know."

**"What are we comfortable not doing?"**
Defines scope. Surfaces what's negotiable. Clarifies the tradeoff.

**Lead with curiosity, not certainty.** Say "Help me understand..." not "Here's what we should do." Say "What am I missing?" not "That won't work because..." Curiosity is not a soft skill. It's the mechanism by which you scale your thinking through the room's intelligence.

Your job is to synthesize the room's thinking, not provide all the answers. Edit ruthlessly. Ask questions that sharpen. Then get out of the way and let the team own the decision.

## Common Mistakes

- **Asking questions you already know the answer to.** That's not curiosity. That's a test. The room feels it and shuts down.
- **Asking too many questions.** Four good questions beat ten scattered ones.
- **Not letting silence sit.** People need time to think. Don't fill the quiet.
- **Answering your own questions.** Ask. Wait. Let the room answer. Your silence is more powerful than your voice.
- **Confusing options with answers.** "Do you want A or B?" is not a question. "What would make this work?" is.

## Try This Week

In your next meeting where a decision needs to happen, try this: Don't give your opinion first. Ask "What problem are we solving?" and listen. Then ask "What would change your mind if you disagreed?" Let the room answer. Only after you've heard everyone ask: "What information would make this obvious?" Watch what happens. The team will own the decision faster. And you'll learn something you didn't know.
