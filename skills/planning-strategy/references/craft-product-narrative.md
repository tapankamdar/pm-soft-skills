# Craft Your Product Narrative

> Use when you need to move a team from debating requirements to rallying around a mission.

## The Gap

All PMs write specs. Great PMs build narratives.

A spec tells people what to build. A story makes them want to build it. When you lead with requirements, people argue about requirements. When you lead with a person — someone real, with a name and a constraint — the debate changes entirely.

## Why It Matters

When you walk into a room with a feature list, you'll get feature-list arguments. Someone will push back on priority. Someone else will debate the timeline. The energy is defensive and abstract.

But when you walk in with a person — the Las Vegas mom running a blog in two-hour windows between school drop-off and pickup, suddenly blocked by a WordPress update — the conversation becomes about solving a problem that matters. The team stops optimizing for specs. They start optimizing for that person's relief. That shift is what separates projects that ship on time and on budget from projects that drag and drown in scope creep.

Narratives work because they make the stakes tangible. Features are abstract. People are real. Your job is to make your audience care about the person so much that they care about the outcome.

## The Framework

Use these three moves to transform a feature into a story:

1. **Name the user.** Not a persona demographic. A person. Give them a name, a constraint, and a context. "The Las Vegas Mom" is stronger than "small business owner." Specificity triggers empathy.

2. **Feel the pain.** Don't describe the friction as a technical problem. Describe what it costs her — time lost, confidence drained, momentum killed. "A WordPress update breaks her publishing flow in the one window she has to write" hits harder than "UX inconsistency."

3. **Describe the magic.** Don't list features. Describe the moment the user feels relief. What does her day look like after your product launches? What becomes possible?

## Common Mistakes

- **Building the persona instead of the person.** Personas are forgettable. People aren't. Use a real name and a real constraint.
- **Focusing on the feature instead of the feeling.** "We built a faster editor" is a feature. "She can now publish her post without her daughter asking 'are you done yet?'" is the narrative.
- **Over-explaining the technical solution.** The user doesn't care how you solve it. She cares that you understood her problem.
- **Leading with the opportunity size.** "This market is $10B" kills the narrative. Start with one person. Scale comes after alignment.
- **Assuming your team knows the user.** Say the name. Say the constraint. Say it again. Repetition embeds narrative.

## Try This Week

Before your next product kickoff or planning meeting, write one sentence: the post your user would share the day after launch.

"Finally. It just works."

"I'm publishing two posts a week now instead of one."

"For the first time, I don't dread this workflow."

If you can't write it, the idea isn't sharp enough. Spend more time with your narrative. The sharpness of your story will determine the sharpness of your execution.
