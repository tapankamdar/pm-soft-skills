# Build a Product Vision That Travels

> Name the user, describe the relief they feel, and set a direction that survives individual features.

## The Gap

You can ship great features, hit milestones, and still feel like your impact has a ceiling. Progress is happening, but it doesn't feel intentional. All PMs generate ideas. Great PMs transform ideas into visions that others can see, feel, and follow.

The job isn't to ship features. The job is to make the direction so clear that every feature decision becomes obvious. Teams move fast without a clear sense of where they're actually headed. PMs who skip the vision end up rebuilding the roadmap constantly because there's no north star to organize around.

## Why It Matters

Without vision, every feature feels like a one-off. With vision, every feature is a chapter in a larger story. Vision doesn't tell you what to build next quarter—it tells you why you're building anything at all.

A vision gives your team permission to say no. It gives your cross-functional partners a reason to believe in you when the execution gets messy. It survives leadership changes, market shifts, and individual priorities. Vision is what outlasts quarterly plans.

When you take time to clarify your vision, you discover what you shouldn't build. You find the constraints that actually matter. You build alignment that feels voluntary, not imposed.

## The Framework

A vision has three elements:

**1. Name the user and their world today** (not the product—the person)
- Who is this for? What do they currently do? What friction do they feel?

**2. Describe the world after** (what relief, possibility, or new power does the user have?)
- What changes when your product exists? What becomes possible that wasn't before?

**3. Set a direction that survives individual features** (the north star)
- This is the outcome, the shift, the new normal. Not a feature. Not a list of capabilities. A direction.

Then test it: **The Sell Tour.** Before finalizing your vision, share it with 5-7 people—peers, engineers, a skeptic. Not to get approval, but to find the gaps. A vision that survives a tough room is a vision that will travel.

Write down every major objection you hear. That's your **Objection List.** For each one, sharpen your response. This doesn't mean talking people out of concerns—it means refining the vision so the concerns are either addressed or understood as acceptable tradeoffs.

Try **The Future Tweet:** Write one sentence your user would share the day after launch. "Finally. It just works." If you can't, the vision isn't sharp enough.

## Common Mistakes

- **Leading with product features.** "We're building a better dashboard" is not vision. "We're helping managers spend 10 hours a week less on status reports" is.
- **Staying abstract.** "Empower users" is too vague. "Give designers the ability to collaborate in real-time without losing their work" is concrete.
- **Skipping the sell tour.** You skip feedback, find blind spots later, and have to reshape the vision mid-execution.
- **No north star.** A vision that's just a list of user benefits is a roadmap pretending to be vision.
- **Not testing the objection list.** When real resistance surfaces later, you don't have responses ready.

## Try This Week

Write your vision in three sentences: the user and their world today, the world after, the north star. Read it out loud. Does it feel like a direction you'd follow for 12 months? Now book one 1:1 with a skeptic—someone who doesn't automatically agree. Listen. Revise. You've just started the sell tour.
