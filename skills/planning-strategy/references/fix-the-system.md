# Fix the System, Not Just the Symptom

> If your team keeps missing, it's not the team. It's the system. Great PMs zoom out and fix the root.

## The Gap

When a team misses a deadline, the instinct is to push harder: more standups, more accountability, more urgency. But if the same problems keep appearing — scope that keeps expanding, priorities that keep shifting, outcomes nobody can define — the solution is not more pressure. It is a different system.

> "All PMs fix delays. Great PMs fix the system. The process is the product. If the system is broken, the features will be too." — Tapan Kamdar

## Why It Matters

Teams are not lazy. They are trapped. When a PM keeps patching the same breakdowns without addressing the root, the team learns that their environment is unpredictable. They start to protect themselves — sandbagging estimates, avoiding stretch commitments, losing initiative.

The best PMs treat process like a product. They observe where things break down, diagnose root causes, run experiments, and improve the operating system their team works in. Your output reflects your system. Build it with care.

## Common System Failures

**Endless scope creep**
- Root cause: Success criteria were never defined, or weren't enforced.
- Fix: Write the outcome in the PRD before writing the features. Require a tradeoff decision when scope is added.

**Misaligned priorities**
- Root cause: The roadmap has too many top priorities, or leaders can override it without consequence.
- Fix: Maintain a single ranked list. When a new item enters, an old item must exit. Make the tradeoff explicit and documented.

**No clarity on outcomes**
- Root cause: The team ships features without knowing what "good" looks like.
- Fix: Every initiative ships with a success metric, a measurement plan, and a review date.

**Recurring blockers between teams**
- Root cause: Dependencies aren't identified early; escalation paths are unclear.
- Fix: Surface cross-team dependencies in planning. Assign a named owner for each. Review weekly.

**Post-launch drift**
- Root cause: The team moves to the next thing before learning from the last.
- Fix: Schedule a launch retrospective at 2 and 6 weeks post-launch. Review the outcome metric against the goal before starting the next cycle.

## The System Diagnosis Approach

When you notice a recurring problem, resist the impulse to fix the instance. Instead:

1. **Name the pattern.** "This is the third sprint we've missed because of late design handoffs."
2. **Map the failure chain.** Trace back to where the breakdown began. Was it unclear requirements? A dependency that wasn't flagged? A process that assumed more lead time than we have?
3. **Propose a structural fix.** Not "let's try harder." Something you can observe and measure — a changed process, a new artifact, a different meeting cadence.
4. **Run the fix for 2 sprints.** Evaluate. Adjust.

## Distinction: Problem vs. System

| Problem-level response | System-level response |
|---|---|
| "We shipped late — let's work nights to catch up." | "We shipped late — let's understand why estimates miss by 30% and change how we scope." |
| "The design handoff broke down — let's get more meetings." | "The handoff broke down — let's define the handoff spec and include it in the sprint checklist." |
| "The team seems unmotivated — let's do a team dinner." | "The team seems unmotivated — let's check whether goals are clear, work is meaningful, and blockers are removed." |

## Try This Week

Identify one recurring frustration on your team: a meeting that always runs long, a process that always slips, a handoff that always breaks. Don't fix the most recent instance. Ask: what system assumption is producing this outcome? Then propose one change to the system — even a small one — and observe the results.
