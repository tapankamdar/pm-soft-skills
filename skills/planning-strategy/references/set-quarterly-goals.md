# Set Quarterly Goals That Stick

> Use PACE to move from vague direction to shared commitment—and leading indicators that show whether you're actually tracking.

## The Gap

Most teams organize their roadmap around projects: discrete, bounded, done. All PMs set goals. Great PMs think in themes—a problem space your team commits to for a quarter, with multiple potential bets, learning, and room to pivot when signals change.

A team that can't say their goal in one sentence doesn't have a goal—they have a direction. Direction is not enough. The team needs to believe the goal is achievable, understand what success looks like the same way, and know which leading indicators show they're on track before the quarter ends.

## Why It Matters

Quarterly goals without alignment create invisible work. Each person assumes success looks different. Half the team ships what's on the roadmap. Half the team navigates obstacles and adapts. You end up checking the box at the end of the quarter and feeling like you accomplished nothing.

When goals are organized around themes instead of projects, you create narrative. You answer "what are we working on" in a way that makes sense to engineers, designers, and executives. One project fails? You learn and pivot under the theme without abandoning the direction. Themes survive individual features. Projects don't.

## The Framework

Use **PACE** to set and test every quarterly goal:

**Purpose:** Why does this goal matter? Connect it to the user (what friction does it solve?) and the business outcome (what does this unlock?).

**Alignment:** Does your whole team understand the goal the same way? Test it. Ask different team members to describe what success looks like. If they answer differently, you're not aligned.

**Commitment:** Do the people responsible for achieving the goal actually believe it's achievable? A goal without belief is a wish. This is where you surface fears, constraints, and dependencies—not to talk people out of them, but to address them.

**Execution:** What are your leading indicators? How will you know you're on track before the end of the quarter? Don't wait until week 12 to discover you're behind.

Then structure your roadmap around a theme. Example: Instead of "Launch checkout v2," your theme is "Reduce friction for first-time buyers." Multiple bets live under that theme. When one doesn't work, you learn and pivot—without abandoning the theme.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing projects with goals.** "Build feature X" is a project. "Reduce checkout abandonment by 15%" is a goal.
- **Missing belief.** If the team doesn't think the goal is achievable, it's not a goal—it's pressure. Surface the doubt early.
- **Vague success criteria.** "Improve user experience" doesn't measure. "Increase task completion rate to 85% by end of Q2" does.
- **No leading indicators.** You discover you're off track in week 11, not week 2. Define early signals that tell you when to adjust.
- **Skipping the why.** If your team doesn't understand why this goal matters to users, they'll optimize for shipping instead of impact.

## Try This Week

Take your current or upcoming quarterly goal. Write it in one sentence. Now ask three different team members to describe what success looks like. If the answers differ, you've found your alignment gap. Schedule a 30-minute sync to align on purpose, success criteria, and leading indicators. You'll save weeks of misalignment later.
