# Delegate with Ownership

> Use when you need to shift from being the person who does the work to being the person who enables others to do great work.

## The Gap

All PMs assign tasks. Great PMs give ownership.

Assignment is giving someone a task. Delegation is giving someone a problem to own. The PM who does the work has a team. The PM who enables the work has a force multiplier.

## Why It Matters

There's a subtle but critical difference between "please run this analysis" and "this insight will determine our Q3 strategy — own this." In the first case, the team member executes. In the second case, they think like a decision-maker.

When a team member truly owns a problem, their posture changes. They bring solutions you didn't ask for. They anticipate blockers. They care about the outcome, not just the task. They ask the questions that reframe the work.

This is the shift from doing to enabling. It's the difference between a team that executes your vision and a team that becomes your thinking partners. And it compounds: great team members attract other great team members. People want to work where they feel genuinely trusted.

The cost of keeping work close? You become the bottleneck. Your team learns to wait for your input instead of bringing you their solution. Projects slow. You get pulled into details. You stop thinking strategically.

## The Framework

Ownership happens at the moment of handoff. Here's how to do it:

1. **Be clear about the outcome, not the process.** "I need you to own whether we go build, buy, or partner for this capability. Here's what we're trying to solve. Here's the decision criteria. Bring me the recommendation with your reasoning."

2. **Set the scope and the constraints.** "I need this by next Thursday. You have three people and a $50K budget. What will you deliver?" Now they're designing the work, not just executing it.

3. **Name them publicly.** When the work lands, don't say "the team did great work." Say "Jordan's analysis uncovered the insight that changed our approach." Ownership is felt when the owner is seen.

4. **Show up as a resource, not a reviewer.** When they hit a blocker, ask "what are you stuck on?" not "what did you do?" Be the blocker-remover, not the gate-keeper.

5. **Trust them to fail small and iterate.** If they own the problem, they also own the approach. Let them take a different path than you would. Most of the time, they'll teach you something.

## Common Mistakes

- **Handing off work but keeping the decision.** This creates confusion and kills ownership. You either own it or they do.
- **Changing the criteria mid-stream.** If the decision criteria shifts, reset the conversation. Don't let them guess.
- **Giving feedback on their approach instead of their outcome.** "That's not how I would have done it" is not feedback. "The deadline slipped because we underestimated scope" is.
- **Taking back work when they struggle.** Struggle is part of growth. Support them through it instead.
- **Forgetting to recognize the ownership.** Publicity matters. It signals to the whole team who the real decision-makers are.

## Try This Week

Find one project or problem that you've been carrying that someone on your team is ready to own. Have the conversation this week.

Be explicit: "I'm going to move this from my plate to yours. It's yours to own — you decide the approach, you bring me the recommendation, you own the outcome. I'm here if you hit blockers."

Then step back. Let them own it.
