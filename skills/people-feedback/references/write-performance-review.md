# Write a Performance Review That Matters

> Use when it's review time and you need to create a document that tells the person how they're actually doing and opens the door to growth.

## The Gap

All PMs write performance reviews. Great PMs write reviews that help people see themselves clearly.

A good review describes what happened. A great review explains the significance. "She ran the Q3 planning offsite" is description. "She ran the Q3 planning offsite and aligned 5 teams on the roadmap without a single escalation" is significance. The second one explains why it mattered.

## Why It Matters

Your review becomes the mirror through which people see themselves. If your language is weak, even if you intend a strong rating, the calibrator — the person who actually decides the rating — will see weak evidence and rate accordingly. You're not just evaluating the person. You're creating the language that determines how the person is perceived across the organization.

Recency bias is real. The work from July gets forgotten. The presentation last month becomes the whole story. Your job is to hold the whole year. Use specific examples from across the cycle, not just the last month.

There's also a critical distinction between performance and potential. Performance is what they delivered this year. Potential is what they could deliver next year. Don't conflate them. A great performer might not be ready for the next level. A junior person with high potential might not be crushing this level yet.

The review is a mirror, not a verdict. It's an opportunity for the person to see what the organization sees and decide what they want to do about it.

## The Framework

Structure your review this way:

1. **Start with evidence, not conclusions.** Not "she's a strong communicator" but "she ran the Q3 planning offsite and aligned 5 teams on the roadmap without a single escalation." Let the evidence speak.

2. **Separate performance from potential.** Use different sections:
   - **Performance:** What did they deliver this year? What impact did it have? Use metrics where you can — user satisfaction, launch cadence, quality measures.
   - **Potential:** What's their ceiling? What would they need to develop to reach it?

3. **Use specific, behavioral examples.** Not personality, behavior. Not "she's detail-oriented" but "she caught the edge case in the API contract that would have caused a production incident. She documented it and walked the engineer through the fix."

4. **Name the themes across the year.** If you see a pattern — "she solved three cross-team dependencies by facilitating alignment meetings" — call it out. That's a strength.

5. **Be honest about what's not working.** If performance is below expectations, name it clearly. "She's shipped three features this year and all three missed their impact targets. The gap is clarity upfront about success metrics." That's not mean. That's honest.

6. **Close with a clear forward look.** Not "keep up the good work" but "next year, we need to see you shipping faster. That means bringing us early alerts on scope creep, not course-correcting in month three."

## Common Mistakes

- **Using vague language to soften a lower rating.** "Could lean in more on cross-team collaboration" is so soft the message dies. "Owns her piece, delegates the dependencies" is clearer and more honest.
- **Letting recency bias dominate.** That great project in February got forgotten. Look back. Make it visible.
- **Confusing performance with potential.** High performers aren't always ready for promotion. Don't mix the messages.
- **Writing a good review but using weak language in the rating justification.** The calibrator sees the justification first. Make sure your language there is as clear as your detailed comments.
- **Avoiding the difficult truth.** "She's a nice person but she's not closing deals" is harder to write than "strong culture fit." But it's necessary.
- **Assuming the person knows what they did well.** Make it explicit. People often underestimate their impact.

## Try This Week

Draft the review using the structure above. Pull specific examples from across the year, not just the last month. Separate performance from potential. Let the evidence speak.

Before submitting, ask yourself: If a calibrator only read my evidence section, would they reach the same conclusion about the rating that I intend? If not, your language isn't strong enough yet.
