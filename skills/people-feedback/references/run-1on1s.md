# Run 1:1s That Matter

> Use when you want your 1:1s to stop being status updates and start being the place where real work gets done.

## The Gap

All PMs have 1:1s. Great PMs use them to build trust and solve the hard problems.

A 1:1 that's a status update is a waste of time. You can get that from a Slack message. A 1:1 should be the place where your team member brings the things they can't say in a room. The questions they wouldn't ask in front of peers. The confusion they're sitting with. The opportunity they see but aren't sure how to raise.

## Why It Matters

Your team members have things they're thinking about but not saying. Maybe they're stuck on a decision and don't want to look indecisive. Maybe they're seeing a gap in strategy but don't want to challenge the leader. Maybe they're trying to figure out if they want to stay in role or try something different.

If your 1:1 is structured around task updates, those things never surface. And you miss the insight, the opportunity, and the chance to build real trust. More importantly: your team member is carrying that thing alone instead of getting support.

The best 1:1s are the ones where the person walks in with something real and walks out with clarity or support or permission to try something. Those are the conversations that change how your team works and how your people grow.

## The Framework

Before the 1:1:

1. **Let them own the agenda.** Send a message: "What's on your mind that I should know about?" This is their time. Let them shape it.

2. **You bring one hard question.** Not a performance review question. A real question. "Where do you want to go next in your career?" or "What's something you'd normally avoid talking about in a group setting?" or "Is this role giving you what you need?"

During the 1:1:

3. **Start with their agenda, not yours.** If they have something, hear it first. Status comes after.

4. **Ask: "What's on your mind that I might not know about?"** This question opens doors. People will tell you the real thing if you give them permission.

5. **If they're stuck, ask before you solve.** "What have you considered?" before "Here's what I'd do." Let them think out loud.

6. **Take notes on the hard questions.** Not to build a case against them. To create clarity. Clarity prevents misunderstanding.

After the 1:1:

7. **Send a brief summary of what you aligned on.** "We talked about you potentially taking on the analytics work by Q2. We agreed you'd spend two weeks learning the codebase and then we'll sync on whether it's a good fit." This alone prevents more misalignment than any amount of extra clarification.

## Common Mistakes

- **Treating it as a status update.** If your 1:1s are "what did you ship this week," you're wasting their time. Jira can tell you that.
- **Bringing your agenda instead of letting them bring theirs.** If you always control the conversation, they'll stop bringing the real things.
- **Solving the problem before they finish thinking.** Let them sit in the discomfort for a moment. They often solve it themselves.
- **Skipping the follow-up summary.** People forget what they aligned on. Write it down. One paragraph. It prevents confusion.
- **Asking surface questions when you see deeper things.** If someone seems stuck, ask about it. "You seem like something's off" opens more than "how's work?"
- **Not protecting the time.** Cancel the 1:1 and your team member learns it's not a priority. Protect that time like you'd protect a customer call.

## Try This Week

For your next 1:1, send the message first: "What's on your mind that I should know about?" Then go in curious.

Don't bring your agenda. Let them bring theirs. Listen for the thing they're not saying directly. Ask one hard question. Take notes. Send the summary after.

Notice what happens when you shift from controlling the conversation to holding space for real thinking.
