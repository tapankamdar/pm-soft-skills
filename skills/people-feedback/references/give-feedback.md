# Give Feedback

> Use when someone's work or behavior isn't matching expectations and you need to create clarity without killing the relationship.

## The Gap

All PMs give feedback. Great PMs give feedback that changes behavior.

Most feedback is vague enough that the recipient can rationalize it away. "You need to communicate more" gets filed in the "nice to hear" folder and nothing changes. Great feedback is specific, timely, and focused on behavior — not personality.

## Why It Matters

Feedback delayed is feedback that loses impact. The moment matters. If someone misses a deadline on Monday and you wait until the Friday check-in, they've already moved on. If they bombed a presentation and you say nothing, they'll give the same presentation next time.

More importantly: feedback early prevents problems from metastasizing. When the gap is small, the fix is easier. When you wait, the gap grows. A small communication issue becomes a pattern. A pattern becomes a reputation. A reputation becomes a performance issue that's much harder to fix.

The other cost of avoiding hard feedback: you're protecting yourself, not them. If you know someone's not meeting expectations and you don't tell them, you're not being kind. You're setting them up for a surprise at review time. You're stealing their chance to correct course.

## The Framework

Use this structure to make feedback that sticks:

1. **Ask permission first, especially if it's unexpected.** "Can I share an observation?" gives them a heads-up and opens them to hearing it. It also respects their autonomy.

2. **Separate the behavior from the person.** "When you did X, the impact was Y" — not "You are always Z." This makes it about the action, not about who they are.
   - Instead of: "You're not a detail person."
   - Try: "When you presented without reviewing the deck with the designer first, the stakeholder got confused about the interaction model. Next time, let's do a 30-minute sync before the room."

3. **Make it a conversation, not a lecture.** After you share your observation, ask: "What's your read on this?" or "What would have changed the outcome?" Their perspective matters. Maybe you're seeing it wrong.

4. **Be clear about what success looks like.** Don't just tell them what's wrong. Show them the path forward. "Here's what I need to see from you" is more useful than "stop doing that."

5. **Follow up.** Give feedback, then watch. If the behavior shifted, acknowledge it. If it didn't, that's data you need — and it might be a bigger conversation.

## Common Mistakes

- **Softening the message so much the point gets lost.** "You know, sometimes it helps to think about the user's perspective" is so soft nobody knows they did something wrong.
- **Framing it as their personality.** "You're not collaborative" feels like a character attack. "In the brainstorm, you presented the solution before asking for input from the engineer" is fixable behavior.
- **Giving feedback in a group.** Handle it 1:1. Respect their dignity.
- **Avoiding the hard feedback because the relationship feels fragile.** If the relationship is fragile, it's more important to be honest, not less. Honesty builds trust.
- **Letting them guess what the impact was.** "Your slides looked rough" is vague. "I got three questions from exec sponsors about whether we've thought through the edge case you mentioned casually. Let's prep those answers next time."

## Try This Week

Think of someone on your team where there's a gap between expectations and performance.

Have the conversation this week. Open with permission. Describe the specific behavior and its impact. Ask for their read. Show them what success looks like. Then follow up in two weeks to see if the shift happened.
