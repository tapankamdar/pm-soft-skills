# Ask Better Questions

> You're not expected to have all the answers. You're expected to ask better questions. PMs are learning engines, not answer machines.

## The Gap

PMs are often evaluated on their ability to have the answer: the strategy, the recommendation, the call. But the best PMs are often the ones who ask the question no one else thought to ask — and then listen carefully to the answer.

> "All PMs have answers. Great PMs ask questions. Lead with curiosity, not certainty. Guide teams through ambiguity. Unlock insights through inquiry. Better questions lead to sharper decisions." — Tapan Kamdar

## Why It Matters

The PM who always has an answer creates a dynamic where the team defers instead of thinks. The PM who asks good questions creates a dynamic where the team surfaces its collective knowledge — and the PM synthesizes it into better decisions.

Questions also serve a second purpose: they signal humility and intellectual honesty. When a PM asks a genuine question in a room full of engineers, designers, and data scientists who each know something the PM doesn't, it unlocks information that would otherwise stay siloed. The question is the unlock mechanism.

## The Three Types of Questions PMs Should Ask

**1. Problem questions** — What is the actual problem here?
- "What problem are we solving?"
- "Who is experiencing this and how often?"
- "What does the current workaround look like?"
- "What's the cost of not solving it?"

These questions anchor the work to a real user need before solutions are discussed.

**2. Assumption questions** — What are we taking for granted?
- "What's the riskiest assumption in this plan?"
- "What would have to be true for this to fail?"
- "Have we talked to anyone who disagrees with this view?"
- "What would change our minds?"

These questions surface hidden risks and prevent overconfidence. They are especially valuable in planning, roadmap reviews, and before launches.

**3. Learning questions** — What are we discovering as we go?
- "What's the smallest test that gets us a signal?"
- "What did we learn from this that we didn't expect?"
- "What would we do differently if we started over?"
- "What does the data say versus what we believed?"

These questions keep the team in a learning posture rather than a defending posture.

## How to Ask Questions Well

**Ask with intent, not interrogation.** A question asked with genuine curiosity lands very differently than one asked to expose a flaw. Know which one you're doing. Aim for the former.

**Ask one question at a time.** Multi-part questions are hard to answer well. "What's the problem, who is it affecting, and what are the tradeoffs?" is three questions. Ask one. Wait for the answer.

**Follow the answer.** The most valuable questions are often not the first one — they're the follow-up. "Interesting — what makes you say that?" or "Can you say more about that?" extracts more insight than any prepared question.

**Ask questions you don't know the answer to.** If you already know what you think, you're not asking a question — you're leading a witness. The best discovery questions come from genuine uncertainty.

## Using Questions in Different Situations

**User interviews:** Don't ask "do you like X?" Ask "tell me about the last time you tried to do X." Lead with behavior and experience, not opinion.

**With engineers during planning:** "What's the thing that could go wrong that we haven't talked about yet?" This question almost always produces something valuable.

**In executive reviews:** "What would you need to see to change your view on this?" This surfaces the criteria, not just the objection.

**In team retrospectives:** "What's the thing we're all feeling but haven't said out loud?" Gives voice to the unsaid.

## Common Mistakes

- **Asking rhetorical questions.** "Shouldn't we be thinking about the user?" is not a question. It's a comment in disguise. Ask what you actually want to know.
- **Answering your own question.** "What do you think about X? I think Y..." defeats the purpose. Ask, then go quiet.
- **Avoiding questions in front of leadership.** Many PMs stop asking questions in senior rooms because they worry it signals weakness. It signals the opposite.

## Try This Week

In your next product conversation — a planning session, a spec review, a user interview — commit to asking three questions before making any recommendation. Notice what you learn that you wouldn't have known otherwise.
