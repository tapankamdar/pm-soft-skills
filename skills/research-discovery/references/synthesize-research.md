# Synthesize Research Into Strategy

> Use when you're drowning in data and need to turn interviews, feedback, and metrics into a clear point of view.

## The Gap

All PMs gather research. Great PMs synthesize it into strategy.

Most PMs drown in research because they try to read everything before synthesizing. You end up with a huge pile of notes and no idea what it means. Synthesis isn't summarizing. It's taking 50 data points and arriving at a point of view.

The output of synthesis is not a summary. It's a perspective. It's not "here's what we learned" but "here's what we should build next because of what we learned."

## Why It Matters

Research without synthesis is just noise. You've got 50 interview snippets, 20 survey responses, three months of usage data, and feedback from your biggest customer. So what? What do you do with it?

Great PMs lead with synthesis, not data. They don't walk into a room with a stack of interviews and say "read this." They walk in with a clear insight and use the data as proof. "Users are abandoning carts because they can't see shipping cost upfront. Here are five interviews where customers said exactly that, and our data shows 40% of checkouts get abandoned on the shipping screen."

That's synthesis. You took noise and made it narrative.

The cost of bad synthesis: you misread the market. You over-index on one loud customer or one data point. You build the wrong thing. You ship it and discover six months later that your interpretation was wrong.

## The Framework

**Step 1: Start with the question, not the data.**

Before you read anything, write the question you're trying to answer. Not "what do our users think?" but "should we build a collaborative editing feature or a faster import tool?" or "are we losing customers because of price, quality, or support?"

Start with the question. It shapes how you read the data.

**Step 2: Group insights by theme, not by source.**

Don't organize by "interview 1, interview 2, interview 3." Organize by "pain points" or "competitive gaps" or "workflow changes users want." Group the data into buckets that answer your question.

Use AI to help here. "Here are 50 interview snippets. Group them by theme and surface the top 5 insights." AI will spot patterns across qualitative data faster than you can.

**Step 3: Surface the anomalies.**

Look for the data points that don't fit the pattern. "Most customers love feature X, but three of them hate it because..." That's interesting. Those three might be a new segment or they might see something the majority missed.

**Step 4: Ask: What would change our minds?**

If your synthesis is "users want faster loading," ask: what evidence would change that conclusion? If you found that 80% of users never use the loading-intensive features, would you still think speed is the priority? That question surfaces your most important assumptions.

**Step 5: Write your point of view in one paragraph.**

Not a summary. A perspective. "Users are abandoning our product 72 hours after signup because onboarding doesn't teach them the core workflow. They watch videos but need to practice. We should add an in-product simulation before the live product."

That's synthesis. It answers the question. It gives direction.

**Step 6: Show the evidence that led you there.**

Now you add the data. Not to prove you're smart, but to let others follow your thinking. "This insight came from interviews with 12 high-churn users, confirmed by our cohort data showing drop-off on day 3, and validated by our competitors' onboarding flows which include interactive tutorials."

## Common Mistakes

- **Trying to include all the data in your synthesis.** If it didn't inform your point of view, it doesn't belong in the synthesis. Keep it focused.
- **Changing your story based on whoever's in the room.** If one investor cares about retention, you suddenly emphasize retention. But your data says the real insight is engagement. Stick to what the data says.
- **Over-weighting one customer or one data point.** A big customer is loud, but they're not representative. Separate signal from noise.
- **Summarizing instead of synthesizing.** "Users said X, Y, and Z" is summary. "We should prioritize X because of why Y and Z aren't relevant right now" is synthesis.
- **Forgetting to say what you're *not* building.** Synthesis means choices. "We're not doing a collaboration feature yet because speed is the bottleneck right now." That's as important as what you are doing.

## Try This Week

Take your current pile of research. Write down the question you're trying to answer. Organize the data by theme. Then write one paragraph: your point of view. Not a summary. A recommendation.

If you can't write it, you need more data or different data. That's useful information. But don't write five pages of synthesis because you're uncertain. Work your way to certainty, then write it clear.
